The Harappa town planning has amazed archaeologists worldwide.

So what is Harappa?

Harappa Civilization, also known as the Indus Valley Civilization, was a Bronze Age civilization that lasted from 3300 BC to 1300 BC. It is located in modern-day Afghanistan to Pakistan and northwest India. The civilization's territory stretched over 1,500 km (900 miles) along the Indus River. The two best-known excavated cities of this culture are Harappa and Mohenjo-daro, both located in modern-day Pakistan.

Features of Harappa:

Large cities divided into two parts:

The ‘Citadel’ which is built on high ground. The town to the east was the main area of the city, and it was a residential area, which was surrounded by a massive brick wall.

Streets

The cities were divided into rectilinear grids divided by roads which intersected at right angles, encircled by fortifications, with each block containing houses and public wells.

Materials used to build the houses:

  • Burnt bricks
  • Sun-dried bricks
